What are Lan Speed Issues?

The customer may call in complaining about no connection, slow speeds, or intermittent. The symptoms may be temporarily eased by powercycling the equipment or disconnecting/reconnecting ethernet cables or devices.

What are the visual indicators?

The lan speed entry on the radio may be blank or list something other than 1000 Full or on some equipment 100 Full. Cambium radios list lan speed as “Ethernet Status” on the status page. Ubiquity AC list lan speed as “LAN SPEED” bottom left of the main status page. If you see no indication, “unplugged”, “plugged”, 10 Full, 10 Half, 100 Half, 1000 Half or see the lan speed entry flickering there may be an issue.

What does Lan Speed indicate?

Lan Speed is a negotiation between the radio and the end item, typically a router or less often, a computer. The radio and end item discuss between them what their max connection speed is, then when determined, they lock it in. Any part of the physical connection between the radio and the end item may cause a lan speed issue. If the physical connection is degraded, the lan speed may state a lower transmission speed, or it may indicate Half instead of Full. If lan speed indicates Half then that means instead of the typical 2 way communication simultaneously, data can only go one way at any given time.

How do I troubleshoot this?

When you have a lan speed issue the cause could be any part of the physical connection including the radio and end item (router/laptop). To troubleshoot you’ll want to swap as many of the links in the chain as you can. This typically means bypassing the end item and swapping out the ethernet cables. You may also try things like moving the ethernet from the wan port to a lan port to see if you get a bridge mac where previously you didn’t. If the customer goes out and purchases a new router you may treat this as bypassing the router and troubleshoot the other links.

1. SM Lan Speed indicates unplugged/plugged/Half (10/Half 100/Half 1000/Half) or is flapping

2. We have powercycled POE and router

3. We have swapped out the ethernet cables and also bypassed the end item

4. We have attempted to force the lan speed to negotiate 100 in the SM and then rebooted the SM to verify the changes stick

5. If end item is Mikrotik, then attempt to connect to ports 2-5, if any of these ports resolves lan issue then STS can reprogram that port to WAN - escalate to STS

6. If you have done your best to eliminate possible issues with all the links in the chain, this may require a truck roll. Please reach out to your leadership.


Make sure that if a customer calls in and you resolve the issue by powercycling that you document this thoroughly. If the customer calls in and we document this process 3 times in a 30-45 day period then we may very well have an issue there on site with a piece of equipment. Engage your leadership and see about getting this customer a free truck roll.

  2. Hidden under the LTE Specific Tab is the LTE KMZ, import this into your GEarth to be able to see the LTE deployments and the sectors. Each tower has 3 sectors, ALPHA, BETA, GAMMA. Each sector, when clicked on in the KMZ shows 2 PCI numbers. PCI=Physical Cell ID. If a cx geographical location and sector connection don't match, that's a problem.

  3. Cx info shows up in the lookup, but it says NO Management APN. Make sure authentication lookup says they are authed. Start by closing the IMEI and IMSI packages in BM and making new ones with the same info. If you are speaking with the cx, have them p/.c.   4. Bouncing: Bouncing is a last resort, if nothing above or anything else you've tried has worked to restore management APN or provisioning, bounce.

  5. Shaping lookup is useful when the UE pulls speeds, but a router can't, this checks what procera has listed for the router's queues. if this is wrong and a package refresh and total p/c does not fix this, send it up.

Part 2

BEC

  1. When looking up an IMSI in BEC, the useful info can be attained by clicking the listing, clicking the "Device" dropdown, and selecting analysis. On the analysis page, you'll see a variety of options. First thing to check will always be the "4g LTE" section. This holds the RF Conditions for the Cx UE. Generally these should fall under into the following ranges:

    RSRP: -70 to -100
    SINR: 5 to 30
    RSRQ: -3 to -12
    RSSI: -45 to -75

  2. Network Info this shows a few tidbits, but the really important one is going to be the PCI. the PCI or Physical Cell ID is the numerical ID of the Sector the cx is connected to.(see Part 1's LTETOOL section 2)

  3. GPS LAT and LONG are very useful to get the precise location of the UE.

  4. RF HISTORY is a great way to see the changes in the CX RF conditions over time.

  5. the speed report tab: this allows you to run speedtests to the CX UE. Warning, this does not take into account the traffic use from the cx router.

Management Page

  1. On the UE's management page, you can do everything you can do in BEC and More. Status > 3G/4G-LTE status This is where you'll find the RF conditions for the radio, the current PCI, and the channel and width of the LTE connection.

  2. Status > GPS Status

    This is where you'll find the coordinates for the LTE radio both in Degrees Minutes Seconds, and Decimal Degrees.

  3. Status > Statistics

    This is where you'll find the tools to watch traffic both thru the Ethernet port and the radio link.

  4. Status > DHCP Table

    This is where you can find the cx router MAC and 100.

  5. Status > ARP Table

    See DHCP Table.

  6. Configuration > Interface Setup > Internet > LTE PCI Lock

    This is where you can check if LTE engineering has PCI locked a Cx UE to a specific sector. If a cx is on the wrong sector and this is not set up(ex: Cx on KNOBHILL@GAMMA when they should be on SPRINGTOWNREGEN@GAMMA)

  7. Configuration > Maintenance > Diagnostic Tool

    This is where you can find tools like pings, Traceroutes, and the speedtest function.

    SM IP Unusual? Try the LAN Trick.

    In the event that a customer calls in with no internet connection and the AP shows the SM having a 192. IP ADDRESS or anything not a 10. IP ADDRESS, you should perform the "LAN Trick" before escalating this to another department.

    1. Have the customer unplug the POE and Router power cables.
    2. Have the customer unplug BOTH Ethernet cables from the POE. (POE/LAN Cables)
    3. Once the SM shows no longer connected to the AP, have the customer connect the Ethernet cable that comes from the wall (SM) back into the POE port on the POE, and then connect the power cable back into the POE. The LAN port should have nothing connected.
    4. Once the radio has established connection and pulls a valid IP, power-cycle the router and reconnect the LAN cable to the POE.
    5. Verify this connection has been fully restored.

    The SM should connect again to the AP and show a 10. IP ADDRESS.

    1. If it continues to pull a 192. or anything else, unplug the LAN cable from the POE again.
    2. Power-cycle the POE one more time.
    3. When you see the SM connected on the AP, even if it has a 192. IP, connect the Ethernet back in the LAN on the POE.
    4. If it continues to pull anything but a 10. IP ADDRESS, escalate to Lead/Senior Technical Support as needed.

    Buffering Info

    • Buffering has nothing to do with your units Processor speed, Graphics processing power or RAM
    • Buffering has nothing to do with your hardware

    What causes buffering?

    • Slow internet speeds on the users end
    • Wifi signal being weak and or degraded by distance or having to go through walls or other obstacles
    • Other users on your internet network hogging bandwidth
    • The stream sources server being overloaded with too many users
    • The stream sources server having internet issues
    • Your internet provider having fluctuations in their internet service

    How to minimize buffering while streaming:

    • Have at least 10 Mbps actually getting to the unit. 25 Mbps is preferred
    • Hardwire your unit directly to the internet via an Ethernet cable
    • Power-cycle your router once a week
    • Pause a stream for a minute or two to allow it to build up some data
